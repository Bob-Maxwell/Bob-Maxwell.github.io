Manipulator kinematics

The kinematic function of a link is to maintain a fixed relationship between the two joint axes. This relationship can be described with the link length $a$ and the twist $\alpha$. Moreover, the link offset $d$ and the joint angle $\theta$ are two parameters that may be used to describe the nature of the connection between neighboring links.

In a word, if we abstract the two rotating pairs at the end of the connecting rod into two lines, then only four parameters are needed to characterize the transformation relationship between the two lines:

$a_{i-1}$ = the distance from $\hat{Z}_{i-1}$ to $\hat{Z}_i$ measured along $\hat{X}_i$;

$\alpha_{i-1}$ = the angle from $\hat{Z}_{i-1}$ to $\hat{Z}_i$ measured about $\hat{X}_i$;

$d_i$ = the distance from $\hat{X}_{i-1}$ to $\hat{X}_i$ measured along $\hat{Z}_i$;

$\theta_i$ = the angle from $\hat{X}_{i-1}$ to $\hat{X}_i$ measured about $\hat{Z}_i$.

By defining a frame for each link, we have broken the kinematics problem into $n$ subproblems. In order to solve each of these subproblems, namely $T_i^{i-1}$, we will further break each subproblem into four subsubproblems. Each of these four transformations will be a function of one link parameter only and will be simple enough that we can write down its form by inspection:

$$ \begin{equation*} \begin{aligned} P^{i-1} &= T_i^{i-1}~ P^i \\ &= T_R^{i-1}~ T_Q^R~ T_P^Q~ T_i^P~ P^i \\ &= R_X(\alpha_{i-1})~ D_X(a_{i-1})~ R_Z(\theta_i)~ D_Z(d_i)~ P^i \\ &= \text{Screw}_X(a_{i-1},\alpha_{i-1})~ \text{Screw}_Z(d_i,\theta_i)~ P^i \\ &= \begin{bmatrix} 1&0&0&a_{i-1}\\ 0&c\alpha_{i-1}&-s\alpha_{i-1}&0\\ 0&s\alpha_{i-1}&c\alpha_{i-1}&0\\ 0&0&0&1\\ \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} c\theta_i&-s\theta_i&0&0\\ s\theta_i&c\theta_i&0&0\\ 0&0&1&d_i\\ 0&0&0&1\\ \end{bmatrix} \end{aligned} \end{equation*} $$

Inverse Manipulator kinematics

Jacobians: velocities and static forces

Velocity of rigid bodies

For linear velocity, assume {$A$} be the fixed frame, {$B$} be the frame attached to a rigid body, and we wish to describe the motion of $^BQ$ relative to frame {$A$}, then

$$ \begin{equation*} \begin{aligned} ^AV_Q &= \frac{^A\text{d}}{\text{d}t}(^AX_Q) = \frac{^A\text{d}}{\text{d}t}(^AX_B+^{B(A)}X_Q) \\ &= \frac{^A\text{d}}{\text{d}t}(^AX_B) + \frac{^B\text{d}}{\text{d}t}~ ^BX_Q R_B^A \\ &= ^AV_{BORG} + R_B^A~ ^BV_Q \end{aligned} \end{equation*} $$

For rotational velocity, let’s consider the vector $Q$ is constant as viewed from frame {$B$} first, then point $Q$ will have a velocity as seen from {$A$} due to the rotational velocity $^A\Omega_B$, i.e.

$$ ^AV_Q = ^A\Omega_B \times ^AQ $$

In the general case, the vector $Q$ could also be changing with respect to frame {$B$}, thus

$$ \begin{equation*} \begin{aligned} ^AV_Q &= ^A(^BV_Q) + ^A\Omega_B \times ^AQ \\ &= R_B^A~ ^BV_Q + ^A\Omega_B \times R_B^A~ ^BQ \end{aligned} \end{equation*} $$

Consider linear and rotational velocity simultaneously, we can easily get the derivative of a vector in a moving frame as seen from a stationary frame:

$$ ^AV_Q = ^AV_{BORG} + R_B^A~ ^BV_Q + ^A\Omega_B \times R_B^A~ ^BQ $$

Link-to-link velocity propagation

The angular velocity of link $i+1$ is the same as that of link $i$ plus a new component caused by rotational velocity at joint $i+1$. This can be written in terms of frame {$i$} as

$$ \begin{equation*} \begin{aligned} ^i\omega_{i+1} &= ^i\omega_i + R_{i+1}^i \dot{\theta}_{i+1}~ ^{i+1}\hat{Z}_{i+1} \\ &= ^i\omega_i + R_{i+1}^i~ ^{i+1} \begin{bmatrix} 0\\ 0\\ \dot{\theta}_{i+1}\\ \end{bmatrix} \end{aligned} \end{equation*} $$

By premultiplying both sides by $R_i^{i+1}$, we can find the description of the angular velocity of link $i+1$ with respect to frame {$i+1$}:

$$ ^{i+1}\omega_{i+1} = R_i^{i+1}~ ^i\omega_{i+1} = R_i^{i+1}~ ^i\omega_i + \dot{\theta}_{i+1}~ ^{i+1}\hat{Z}_{i+1} $$

The linear velocity of the origin of frame {$i+1$} is the same as that of the origin of frame {$i$} plus a new component caused by rotational velocity of link $i$, so we have

$$ ^iv_{i+1} = ^iv_i + ^i\omega_i \times ^iP_{i+1} $$

Premultiplying both sides by $R_i^{i+1}$, we compute

$$ ^{i+1}v_{i+1} = R_i^{i+1}~ ^iv_{i+1} = R_i^{i+1} (^iv_i + ^i\omega_i \times ^iP_{i+1}) $$

The corresponding relationships for the case that joint $i+1$ is prismatic are

$$ \begin{equation*} \begin{aligned} ^{i+1}\omega_{i+1} &= R_i^{i+1}~ ^i\omega_i \\ ^{i+1}v_{i+1} &= R_i^{i+1} (^iv_i + ^i\omega_i \times ^iP_{i+1}) + \dot{d}_{i+1} ^{i+1}\hat{Z}_{i+1} \end{aligned} \end{equation*} $$

Jacobians and changing a Jacobian’s frame of reference

In the field of robotics, we generally use Jacobians that relate joint velocities to Cartesian velocities of the tip of the arm, for example

$$ ^0\nu = \begin{bmatrix} ^0v\\ ^0\omega\\ \end{bmatrix} = ^0J(\Theta)\dot{\Theta} $$

where $\Theta$ is the vector of joint angles of the manipulator and $\nu$ is a vector of Cartesian velocities.

Given a Jacobian written in frame {$B$}, we might be interested in giving an expression ofr the Jacobian in another frame {$A$}, that is

$$ ^AJ(\Theta)\dot{\Theta} = \begin{bmatrix} ^Av\\ ^A\omega\\ \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} R_B^A & 0\\ 0 & R_B^A\\ \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} ^Bv\\ ^B\omega\\ \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} R_B^A & 0\\ 0 & R_B^A\\ \end{bmatrix}~ ^BJ(\Theta)\dot{\Theta} $$

Static forces in manipulators and Jacobians in the force domain

We will use our usual convention for assigning frames to links. Figure below shows the static forces and moments (excluding the gravity force) acting on link $i$. Summing the forces and setting them equal to zero, we have

$$ ^if_i - ^if_{i+1} = 0 $$

Summing torques about the origin of frame {$i$}, we have

$$ ^in_i - ^in_{i+1} - ^iP_{i+1}\times ^if_{i+1} = 0 $$

To find the joint torque required to maintain the static equilibrium, the dot product of the joint-axis vector with the moment vector acting on the link is computed:

$$ \tau_i = ^in_i^T~ ^i\hat{Z}_i $$

In case that joint $i$ is prismatic, we compute the joint actuator force as

$$ \tau_i = ^if_i^T~ ^i\hat{Z}_i $$

The principle of virtual work allows us to make certain statements about the static case by allowing the amount of this displacement to go to an infinitesimal, that is

$$ \tau\cdot\delta\Theta = \mathcal{F}\cdot\delta\chi $$

where $\mathcal{F}$ is a 6$\times$1 Cartesian force-moment vector acting at the end-effector, $\delta\chi$ is a 6$\times$1 infinitesimal Cartesian displacement of the end- effector, $\tau$ is a 6$\times$1 vector of torques at the joints, and $\delta\Theta$ is a 6$\times$1 vector of infinitesimal joint displacements. Based on the definition of the Jacobian, we have

$$ \tau^T\cdot\delta\Theta = \mathcal{F}^T\cdot\delta\chi = \mathcal{F}^T\cdot J\delta\Theta $$

Cartesian transformation of velocities and static forces

Assume the two frames here are rigidly connected, then we have the velocity transformation:

$$ \begin{bmatrix} ^Bv_B\\ ^B\omega_B\\ \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} R_A^B & -R_A^B~ ^AP_{BORG}\times\\ 0 & R_A^B\\ \end{bmatrix}\begin{bmatrix} ^Av_A\\ ^A\omega_A\\ \end{bmatrix} $$

we can invert this equation:

$$ \begin{bmatrix} ^Av_A\\ ^A\omega_A\\ \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} R_B^A & ^AP_{BORG}\times R_B^A\\ 0 & R_B^A\\ \end{bmatrix}\begin{bmatrix} ^Bv_B\\ ^B\omega_B\\ \end{bmatrix} $$

Similarly, we can write the force-moment transformation:

$$ \begin{bmatrix} ^AF_A\\ ^AN_A\\ \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} R_B^A & 0\\ ^AP_{BORG}\times R_B^A & R_B^A\\ \end{bmatrix}\begin{bmatrix} ^BF_B\\ ^BN_B\\ \end{bmatrix} $$

Manipulator dynamics

Acceleration of a rigid body

The following equation describes the velocity of a vector $^BQ$ as seen from frame {$A$} when the origins are coincident:

$$ \dot{R}_B^A~ ^BQ + R_B^A~ ^BV_Q = \frac{\text{d}}{\text{d}t}(R_B^A~ ^BQ) = ^AV_Q = R_B^A~ ^BV_Q + ^A\Omega_B\times R_B^A~ ^BQ $$

Differentiat it, we get

$$ \begin{equation*} \begin{aligned} ^A\dot{V}_Q &= \frac{\text{d}}{\text{d}t}(R_B^A~ ^BV_Q) + ^A\dot{\Omega}_B\times R_B^A~ ^BQ + ^A\Omega_B\times \frac{\text{d}}{\text{d}t}(R_B^A~ ^BQ) \\ &= (R_B^A~ ^B\dot{V}_Q + \dot{R}_B^A~ ^BV_Q) + ^A\dot{\Omega}_B\times R_B^A~ ^BQ + ^A\Omega_B\times (R_B^A~ ^BV_Q + ^A\Omega_B\times R_B^A~ ^BQ) \\ &= (R_B^A~ ^B\dot{V}_Q + ^A\Omega_B\times R_B^A~ ^BV_Q) + ^A\dot{\Omega}_B\times R_B^A~ ^BQ + ^A\Omega_B\times (R_B^A~ ^BV_Q + ^A\Omega_B\times R_B^A~ ^BQ) \\ &= R_B^A~ ^B\dot{V}_Q + 2 ^A\Omega_B\times R_B^A~ ^BV_Q + ^A\dot{\Omega}_B\times R_B^A~ ^BQ + ^A\Omega_B\times (^A\Omega_B\times R_B^A~ ^BQ) \end{aligned} \end{equation*} $$

Finally, to generalize to the case in which the origins are not coincident, we add one term which gives the linear acceleration of the origin of {$B$}:

$$ ^A\dot{V}_{BORG} + R_B^A~ ^B\dot{V}_Q + 2 ^A\Omega_B\times R_B^A~ ^BV_Q + ^A\dot{\Omega}_B\times R_B^A~ ^BQ + ^A\Omega_B\times (^A\Omega_B\times R_B^A~ ^BQ) $$

A particular case that is worth pointing out is when $^BQ$ is constant, or $^BV_Q = ^B\dot{V}_Q = 0$. In this case, the equation simplifies to

$$ ^A\dot{V}_Q = ^A\dot{V}_{BORG} + ^A\dot{\Omega}_B\times R_B^A~ ^BQ + ^A\Omega_B\times (^A\Omega_B\times R_B^A~ ^BQ) $$

Consider the case in which {$B$} is rotating relative to {$A$} with $^A\Omega_B$ and {$C$} is rotating relative to {$B$} with $^B\Omega_C$, then

$$ ^A\Omega_C = ^A\Omega_B + R_B^A~ ^B\Omega_C $$

By differentiating, we obtain

$$ \begin{equation*} \begin{aligned} ^A\dot{\Omega}_C &= ^A\dot{\Omega}_B + \frac{\text{d}}{\text{d}t}(R_B^A~ ^B\Omega_C) \\ &= ^A\dot{\Omega}_B + ^A\Omega_B\times R_B^A~ ^B\Omega_C + R_B^A~ ^B\dot{\Omega}_C \end{aligned} \end{equation*} $$

Newton’s and Euler’s equation

The figure below shows a rigid body rotating with angular velocity $\omega$ and with angular acceleration $\dot{\omega}$. In such a situation, the moment $N$, which must be acting on the body to cause this motion, is given by Euler’s equation:

$$ \begin{equation*} \begin{aligned} N &= \dot{I}\cdot\omega + I\cdot\dot{\omega} \\ &= \frac{\text{d}}{\text{d}t}\big(\sum_i\sum_j I_{ij}e_ie_j\big)\cdot\omega + I\cdot\dot{\omega} \\ &= \sum_i\sum_j I_{ij}(\dot{e}_ie_j + e_i\dot{e}_j)\cdot\omega + I\cdot\dot{\omega} \\ &= \sum_i\sum_j I_{ij}\big((\omega\times e_i)e_j - e_i(e_j\times\omega) \big)\cdot\omega + I\cdot\dot{\omega} \\ &= (\omega\times I)\cdot\omega - (I\times\omega)\cdot\omega + I\cdot\dot{\omega} \\ &= (\omega\times I)\cdot\omega + I\cdot\dot{\omega} \end{aligned} \end{equation*} $$

Iterative Newton-Euler dynamic formulation

We now consider the problem of computing the torques that correspond to a given trajectory of a manipulator. We assume we know the position, velocity, and acceleration of the joints, $(\Theta,\dot{\Theta}, \ddot{\Theta})$. With this knowledge, and with knowledge of the kinematics and the mass-distribution information of a robot, we can calculate the joint torques required to cause this motion.

Outward iterations to compute velocities and accelerations

The “propagation” of rotational velocity from link to link is given by

$$ ^i\omega_{i+1} = ^i\omega_i + R_{i+1}^i\dot{\theta}_{i+1}~ ^{i+1}\hat{Z}_{i+1} $$

By differentiating, we obtain

$$ \begin{equation*} \begin{aligned} ^i\dot{\omega}_{i+1} &= ^i\dot{\omega}_i + \dot{R}_{i+1}^i\dot{\theta}_{i+1}~ ^{i+1}\hat{Z}_{i+1} + R_{i+1}^i\ddot{\theta}_{i+1}~ ^{i+1}\hat{Z}_{i+1} \\ &= ^i\dot{\omega}_i + ^i\Omega_{i+1}\times R_{i+1}^i\dot{\theta}_{i+1}~ ^{i+1}\hat{Z}_{i+1} + R_{i+1}^i\ddot{\theta}_{i+1}~ ^{i+1}\hat{Z}_{i+1} \\ &= ^i\dot{\omega}_i + ^i\omega_i\times\dot{\theta}_{i+1}~ ^{i+1}\hat{Z}_{i+1} + R_{i+1}^i\ddot{\theta}_{i+1}~ ^{i+1}\hat{Z}_{i+1} \end{aligned} \end{equation*} $$

By premultiplying both sides by $R_i^{i+1}$, we obtain the equation for transforming angular acceleration from one link to the next:

$$ ^{i+1}\dot{\omega}_{i+1} = R_i^{i+1}~ ^i\dot{\omega}_i + R_i^{i+1}~ ^i\omega_i\times\dot{\theta}_{i+1}~ ^{i+1}\hat{Z}_{i+1} + \ddot{\theta}_{i+1}~ ^{i+1}\hat{Z}_{i+1} $$

When joint $i+1$ is prismatic, this simplifies to

$$ ^{i+1}\dot{\omega}_{i+1} = R_i^{i+1}~ ^i\dot{\omega}_i $$

The linear acceleration of each link-frame origin is obtained by

$$ ^{i+1}\dot{v}_{i+1} = R_i^{i+1}\big(^i\dot{v}_i + ^i\dot{\omega}_i\times ^iP_{i+1} + ^i\omega_i\times (^i\omega_i\times ^iP_{i+1})\big) $$

For prismatic joint $i+1$, we have

$$ ^{i+1}\dot{v}_{i+1} = R_i^{i+1}\big(^i\dot{v}_i + ^i\dot{\omega}_i\times ^iP_{i+1} + ^i\omega_i\times (^i\omega_i\times ^iP_{i+1})\big) + 2~ ^{i+1}\omega_{i+1}\times\dot{d}_{i+1}~ ^{i+1}\hat{Z}_{i+1} + \ddot{d}_{i+1}~ ^{i+1}\hat{Z}_{i+1} $$

We also will need the linear acceleration of the center of mass of each link, which also can be found by

$$ ^i\dot{v}_{C_i} = ^i\dot{\omega}_i\times ^iP_{C_i} + ^i\omega_i\times(^i\omega_i + ^iP_{C_i}) + ^i\dot{v}_i $$

Here, we imagine a frame {$C_i$} attached to each link, having its origin located at the center of mass of the link and having the same orientation as the link frame {$i$}. The equation above doesn’t involve joint motion at all and so is valid for joint $i+1$, regardless of whether it’s revolute or prismatic.

The force and torque acting on a link

Having computed the linear and angular accelerations of the mass center of each link, we can apply the Newton-Euler equations to compute the inertial force and torque acting at the center of mass of each link. Thus we have

$$ \begin{equation*} \begin{aligned} F_i &= m\dot{v}_{C_i} \\ N_i &= ^{C_i}I\dot{\omega}_i + \omega_i\times ^{C_i}I\omega_i \end{aligned} \end{equation*} $$
